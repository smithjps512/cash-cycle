How it works in practice
You open it on buy day and enter three things: cash to deploy, next bill date (defaults to the 1st of next month), and your safety buffer. The buffer persists across sessions, so you set it once and forget.
The tool computes hold length in business days and classifies the cycle automatically. Short cycle (<7 business days, like this Friday's paycheck) forces MMF-only — no dropdown, no decision, just do it. Medium cycle (7-13 days) opens up MMF plus ETF. Full cycle (14+) unlocks all three tiers. For full cycles you can toggle between Conservative/Balanced/Aggressive splits with one click.
For the candidate analyzer, you enter up to three tickers and hit Analyze per row. Each call hits Claude's API with web search enabled and runs the full screen: market cap (>$10B), beta (<1.3), earnings window check (the critical one — no earnings during your hold), 52-week position, and news flags. Each criterion gets a clear pass/fail with the actual value shown, a 0-5 pass count, and a BUY/CAUTION/AVOID verdict. If you run multiple candidates, the one with the most passes gets flagged as "Top pick" — but it only labels this if there's actually more than one analyzed (no false confidence on a single ticker).
The Execution section gives you the literal list of orders for E*Trade, plus your sell-by date (with a 3-business-day cushion before bills) and the date cash needs to be back in banking. You log the cycle when done, and history accumulates at the bottom.
Two real caveats for you
The analysis is as good as web search results on a given day, which is generally solid for large caps but can miss very recent news. Always sanity-check the numbers against what ETrade shows you — especially the earnings date, since that's the one that can actually bite. If Claude's analysis says "no earnings in window" and ETrade's calendar disagrees, trust E*Trade.
The tool doesn't place trades. By design. It screens, you decide, you execute manually. That separation is a feature — it keeps you in the loop on every transaction and avoids the whole category of "automated trading gone wrong."
